Racial diversity at General Studies lags behind as Columbia College and SEAS lead the Ivy League, Common Data Set analysis shows

By Noah Bernstein

October 27, 2023

Photo by Esmeralda Paredes

The School of General Studies is the second most proportionally white undergraduate school in the Ivy League, while Columbia College and the School of Engineering and Applied Science are the least, according to a Spectator analysis of the schools’ 2022-23 Common Data Sets.

More than 40 percent of the General Studies student body self-identified as white, while just under 29 percent of Columbia College and SEAS’ enrollment self-identified as white.

There are two Common Data Sets—one for Columbia College and SEAS and another for General Studies, which has a separate admissions office—and, taken together, they provide a more comprehensive profile of Columbia’s three undergraduate schools, disclosing self-reported statistics on student demographics, admissions processes, financial aid packages, class sizes, and the number of faculty with terminal degrees.

This year’s datasets mark one of the last benchmarks of the University’s undergraduate diversity before entering a post-affirmative action admissions landscape.

The most recent first-year class is the final cohort to be evaluated under affirmative action following the Supreme Court’s landmark decision in June to strike down the use of race-conscious admissions policies. In August, University President Minouche Shafik announced the creation of an admissions working group to holistically review current admissions policies. The group is set to issue its recommendations in December.

“By standardizing data across universities, the Common Data Set is useful in some forms of measuring and comparing diversity, but it does not always present the complete picture of socioeconomic and experiential diversity that are exemplified by the School of General Studies,” a General Studies spokesperson wrote in a statement to Spectator.

The spokesperson emphasized that General Studies is the only undergraduate school in the Ivy League that does not have full-need financial aid funding. Among all enrolled students at General Studies, 29 percent are the first generation in their family to attend college and 42 percent of domestic students are Pell Grant recipients, according to the spokesperson.

Following a data misreporting scandal, which saw Columbia taken off U.S. News & World Report’s 2022 College Best Rankings, the University released its first edition of Common Data Sets last September. While this is only the second year that Columbia has published its Common Data Sets, all other Ivy League universities have published annual Common Data Sets for at least a decade.

Racial and ethnic diversity

The University published both reports in May—just weeks before the Supreme Court struck down affirmative action on June 29—revealing significant differences between the racial diversity of the General Studies student body and that of Columbia College and SEAS.

Compared to the rest of the Ivy League, Columbia College and SEAS enrolled the least proportionally white student body—only 29 percent of its students are white—with the University of Pennsylvania close behind at 31 percent. On the other end of the spectrum are Dartmouth College, where 47 percent of the student body is white, and General Studies, where more than 40 percent of the students are white.

When considering this fall’s incoming class, Columbia College and SEAS and the University of Pennsylvania remain in the same positions, having the least and second-least proportionally white student bodies in the Ivy League, respectively. On the other side, General Studies and Dartmouth have switched places, as the General Studies first-time first-year student enrollment is 48 percent white while Dartmouth’s is 45 percent.

The Common Data Set uses the term “first-time first-year” to define a school’s first-year class. This notably excludes a significant of number students at General Studies—which was created for nontraditional students, according to the school’s website—who have transferred into the school from community college, are participating in one of Columbia’s undergraduate dual-degree programs, or are restarting their undergraduate education after military or job experience, for example.

These students, who are not included as “first-time first-year” students in the Common Data Set, make up 84 percent of General Studies’ fall 2023 class. Only 77 students fit under the “first-time first-year” definition.

The General Studies admissions office weighed the racial and ethnic identity of applicants as “important” factors in its admissions process, whereas the Columbia College and SEAS admissions office weighed racial identity one tier lower as “considered,” according to their respective datasets.

Though the national impact of the recent Supreme Court decision remains to be seen, other institutions, like the University of California system, have not considered the racial identity of its applicants since 1998, when a statewide ballot initiative banning the practice went into effect.

That year, the percentage of Black first-year students who enrolled at the University of California, Los Angeles—one of the system’s flagship schools—dropped by more than 50 percent, according to legal papers filed in the recent Supreme Court cases against Harvard University and the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. The percentage of Latinx students dropped by a similar proportion.

“To think that [racial disparities] are going to go away because you say that you’re not going to take race into account in any public policy is insane,” former Dean of Students Roger Lehecka, CC ’67, GSAS ’78, said in an interview with Spectator.

In 2017, when the case against Harvard was still in lower federal courts, Harvard hired an economist, David Card, to project what would happen to its class of 2019 if affirmative action were to be struck down. Card’s simulation showed that the percentage of its white population would increase from 40 percent of its total student body to 48 percent, the percentage of Asian students would increase slightly, and the percentage of Black and Hispanic students would decrease.

“Every college admissions dean I know, every academic dean I know, is unhappy with the Supreme Court decision,” Lehecka said. “But, they also know very clearly from the general counsels at each of the institutions that they had better comply … and that whichever institution is most successful at matching what they did this past year is going to get sued.”

During his 19-year tenure as dean of students, Lehecka oversaw Columbia’s process of becoming a coeducational institution in 1983 and significant increases in racial diversity. He retired from the University in 2004 but still co-teaches a seminar on equity in higher education.

“College enrollments have changed, and I think I played some role at Columbia in bringing that about,” Lehecka said. “But if you look at public education in the United States, it’s as segregated as it was when I started college, and I was a freshman 60 years ago.”

The same day the court handed down its ruling, former University President Lee Bollinger published an article in the Economist, praising General Studies as an “innovative” way for universities to “obtain more diverse pools of applicants.”

“It’s a school that prides itself on nontraditional students. That’s why I came,” Zia Kara, GS ’24, an international student and entrepreneur from Canada, said. “I love to hear people’s GS stories, and I value people from different backgrounds.”

However, given the comparatively low racial diversity at General Studies, some Columbia faculty members and students questioned if Bollinger’s characterization of General Studies was realistic.

“It’s a little bit laughable, to be honest,” Kara said. “You’re touting GS as a school to increase and promote diversity when you’re really not.”

Michael Thaddeus, the mathematics professor who exposed Columbia’s misreporting of data to the U.S. News & World Report, critiqued Bollinger’s view on General Studies’ capacity for diversity because of the relatively small financial aid packages awarded to General Studies students.

While Columbia College and SEAS met 99.7 percent of demonstrated need, General Studies met only 59.4 percent of need, according to the most recent Common Data Sets.

“If you’re going to increase the number of minority students by bringing them in through GS, you’re basically saying, ‘Well, we want to have more minorities but bring them in through a mechanism that gives them inferior financial aid,’” Thaddeus said. “That struck me as a pretty terrible way to go about it.”

Gender diversity

Columbia College and SEAS received 43 percent more applications from women than men in the last admissions cycle, with 34,734 and 24,233 applications, respectively, according to the Common Data Set. This follows a national trend of women applying to college at higher rates than men.

“I think, societally, it’s a terrible thing that we have this enormous disparity,” Lehecka said. “It isn’t that fewer women should go to college. It’s that more men should go to college.”

However, the number of women and men who were admitted was approximately the same, leading to a 25 percent lower acceptance rate for women than men who applied to Columbia College and SEAS in the last admissions cycle, the Common Data Set shows.

In the fall 2022 admissions cycle, women were accepted at a 25 percent lower rate than men

Of the 60,374 applications Columbia College and the School of Engineering and Applied Science received in the 2022-23 admissions cycle, less than 3.3 percent of women and around 3.8 percent of people identifying as another gender were accepted while the rate for men was 4.4 percent.

“One element of this that gets much too little attention is you have this disparate high school experience,” Lehecka said. “Women are more successful in high school on the whole than young men are—more of them graduate and more of them apply to college and go to college.”

The 2022-23 Common Data Set for Columbia College and SEAS also includes information on the number of applicants who were of “another gender,” a new addition to the University’s reporting since 2021-22. Of applicants, 1,407 students who were of another gender applied and were accepted at a rate similar to the combined rate of men and women at about 4 percent, according to the Common Data Set.

The new General Studies Common Data Set introduced the same classification but did not include any data.

Admissions

The Common Data Sets also shed light on the University’s first admissions cycle since falling from No. 2 to No. 18 in the U.S. News & World Report Best National University Rankings.

Despite the drop, the number of early and regular decision applications to Columbia College and SEAS in the 2022-23 cycle remained relatively constant compared to 2021-22 at 60,374 applicants. The yield rate—the proportion of admitted students who ultimately enroll—also stayed roughly the same at 65 percent.

In a November 2022 interview with Spectator, Bollinger said that although the U.S. News scandal caused “definite harm” to the University, he had not seen any impact on student applications.

“Despite the discrepancies in the data, I knew what an institution Columbia was, and I knew just how incredible all the resources and the faculty and so on would be if I were to get in,” Maraki Tamarat, CC ’27, who applied regular decision, said.

Since the most recent admissions cycle, Columbia has risen back up to No. 12 in the U.S. News and World Report’s 2023-24 Best National University Rankings and No. 5 in the Wall Street Journal’s 2024 Best Colleges in the U.S. ranking, in which it was previously ranked at No. 16.

The Columbia College and SEAS admissions office “considered” standardized test scores but did not require applicants to submit them, in accordance with the test-optional policy without time limits announced by Columbia Undergraduate Admissions in March. Though General Studies is also test-optional, its admissions office considered standardized test scores as “very important.”

Only 69 percent of the enrolled first-year students at Columbia College and SEAS submitted standardized test scores and only 33 percent did so at General Studies, according to the Common Data Sets.

General Studies and Columbia College and SEAS admitted relatively large percentages of transfer students—enrolling 18 percent and 4 percent of the schools’ respective undergraduate populations through transfers, the highest and second-highest in the Ivy League, according to its Common Data Sets. Transfers account for less than 1 percent of the undergraduate enrollment at Harvard University, Princeton University, Yale University, and Dartmouth College.

Financial aid

The total amount of institutional scholarship funding allocated to General Studies saw minimal gains in 2022-23, increasing only 3.5 percent from the 2021-22 academic year up to $32 million, according to the Common Data Set. The funding stayed relatively stagnant despite widespread calls for the University to improve its financial aid packages for General Studies students—an effort spearheaded by student-run advocacy group Equality for GS.

The student group was founded last year by Charissa Ratliff-D’addario, GS ’23, TC ’25, and has also called on the University to issue General Studies graduates Latin diplomas, like those received by Columbia College graduates, and increase transparency in its admissions process, according to its open letter to Bollinger last September. The organization has since been endorsed by New York City Council member Shaun Abreu, CC ’14.

“Feeling like you belong on campus … ties into the financial ability to go,” Ratliff-D’addario said.

For students at Columbia College and SEAS, federal need-based scholarship funding increased by over 11 percent to $9.7 million, and the amount of institutional aid given by Columbia increased 6 percent to over $198 million in the 2022-23 academic year. Self-help aid—including work-study programs, employment, and student loans from all sources except parental loans—dropped 11 percent to $9 million.

Neither report included any figures on the University’s waitlist process. Other peer institutions, including Yale, Princeton, and the University of Pennsylvania, have released waitlist data for at least a decade.

At the bottom of every page of both of Columbia’s Common Data Sets is a statement that reads, “the information provided is accurate and complete to the best of our knowledge, as derived from available records relied on by the University.”
